>[!theorem]
>Let $T:V\rightarrow W$ and $U:W\rightarrow Z$ be linear transformations.
>Then $U\circ T:V\rightarrow Z$ is also linear. Note: $U\circ T$ sometimes is written as $UT$.
>Thus, the composition of two linear transformations is also linear.

***Proof:*** $x,y\in V, c\in F$. $UT(cx+y)=U(T(cx+y))=U(cT(x)+T(y))=cUT(x)+UT(y)$ therefore $UT$ is linear.

>[!theorem]
>Let $T,U_1,U_2\in\mathcal{L}(V,V)$. Then
>(a) $T(U_1+U_2)=TU_1+TU_2$.
>(b) Composition is associative
>(c) $T\circ \text{Id}_V=\text{Id}_V\circ T=T$
>(d) $c(U_1U_2)=(cU_1)U_2=U_1(cU_2)$ for all $c\in F$

>[!theorem]
>Let $V,W,Z$ be finite dimension vector spaces with ordered bases $\alpha,\beta,\gamma$ respectively. Let $T:V\rightarrow W$, $U:W\rightarrow Z$ be linear.
>Then $$[UT]^\gamma_\alpha=[U]^\gamma_\beta[T]^\beta_\alpha$$ (composition corresponds to matrix multiplication).

>[!theorem]
>Let $V$ and $W$ be finite-dimensional vector spaces having ordered bases $\beta$ and $\gamma$, respectively, and let $T:V\rightarrow W$ be linear. Then, for each $x\in V$, we have
>$$[T(u)]_\gamma=[T]^\gamma_\beta[x]_\beta$$

***Proof*:** Let $x\in V$. Define linear transformations:
$f_x:F\rightarrow V$, $f_x(a)=ax$
$g_x:F\rightarrow W$, $g_x(a)=aT(x)$
	$g_x=T\circ f_x$
$F\xrightarrow{f_x}V, F\xrightarrow{g_x}W, V\xrightarrow{T}W$
Let $\alpha=\{1\}$ be an ordered basis for $F$. Then
$[T(x)]_\gamma=[g_x(1)]_\gamma=[gx]^\gamma_\alpha=[T\circ f_x]^\gamma_\beta=[T]^\gamma_\beta[f_x]^\beta_\alpha=[T]^\gamma_\beta[x]_\beta$
$[f_x]^\beta_\alpha=[f_x(1)]_\beta=[x]_\beta$
