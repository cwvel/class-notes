*Recall:* For a matrix $A$, we define the conjugate transpose $A^*$.

>[!theorem]
>Let $V$ be an inner product space and $y\in V$. Then the map $g:V\rightarrow F$ given by $g(x)=\langle x,y\rangle$ is linear.
>$\quad$
>If $V$ is finite-dimensional, every linear $V\rightarrow F$ is of the form $g(x)=\langle x,y\rangle$ for some $y$.

>[!theorem]
>If $V$ is a finite dimensional inner product space over $F$ and $g:V\rightarrow F$ is linear, then there is a unique $y\in V$ such that $g(x)=\langle x,y\rangle$ for all $x$.

***Proof:*** Show that $y$ exists, then show that it is unique.
1. Show that $y$ exists:
Let $\beta=\{v_1,\dots,v_n\}$ orthonormal basis for $V$, and let $y=\sum_{k=1}^n \overline{g(v_i)}v_i$.
Then $$\langle v_jy\rangle=\langle v_j,\sum_{i=1}^n\overline{g(v_i)}v_i\rangle$$
We can expand the second argument:
$$=\sum_{i=1}^n\langle v_j,\overline{g(v_i)}v_i\rangle v_i=\sum_{i=1}^n g(v_i)\langle v_j,v_i\rangle$$
And since $\langle v_j,v_i\rangle=1$ only if $i=j$ and is $0$ everywhere else,
$$=g(v_j)$$
So the linear transformations $g$ and $f(x)=\langle x,y\rangle=\langle .,y\rangle$ agree on the basis $\beta$, meaning they are equal:
$$g(x)=\langle x,y\rangle\forall x\in V$$
2. Uniqueness:
Suppose $g(x)=\langle x,z\rangle$ for $x\in V$. Then $\langle x,z\rangle=\langle x,y\rangle$ for all $\in V$. By an earlier theorem, this implies $y=z$. So it is unique.

>[!theorem]
>Let $V$ be a finite dimensional inner product space and let $T:V\rightarrow V$ be linear. Then there is a unique and linear $T^*:V\rightarrow V$ such that $$\langle T(x),y\rangle=\langle x,T^*(y)\rangle$$ for all $x,y$, defined as the **adjoint** of $T$.

***Proof:*** For all $y\in V$, the function $g:V\rightarrow V$, $g(x)=\langle T(x),v\rangle$ is linear, since $T\circ\langle .,y\rangle$ linear (both $T$ and $\langle .,y\rangle$ linear). By the theorem from last time then, there is a unique element $T^*(y)\in V$ such that $g(x)=\langle x,T^*(y)\rangle$. Define $T^*:V\rightarrow V$ as the function sending $y$ to $T^*(y)$. 
*Linearity:* Let $y_1, y_2\in V$, $c_1,c_2\in F$. Then
$$\begin{align}\langle x,T^*(c_1y_1+c_2y_2)\rangle&=\langle T(x),c_1y_1+c_2y_2\rangle\\&=\overline{c_1}\langle T(x),y_1\rangle +\overline{c_2}\langle T(x),y_2\rangle\\&=\overline{c_1}\langle x,T^*(y_1)\rangle+\overline{c_2}\langle x,T^*(y_2)\rangle\\&=\langle x,c_1T^*(y_1)+c_2 T^*(y_2)\rangle\\&=\langle x,T^*(c_1y_1+c_2y_2)\rangle\\&=\langle x,c_1T^*(y_1)+c_2T^*(y_2)\rangle
\end{align}$$
for all $x$.
Therefore, $T^*(c_1y_1+c_2y_2)=c_1T^*(y_1)+c_2T^*(y_2)$
*Unique:* Lastly, if $U:V\rightarrow V$ satisfies $\langle T(x),y\rangle=\langle x,U(y)\rangle$, then $\langle x,T^*(x)\rangle=\langle T(x),y\rangle=\langle x,U(y)\rangle$ for all $x,y$. So $T^*=U$.

>[!theorem]
>Let $V$ be a finite dimensional inner product space and $\beta$ be an orthonormal basis. If $T:V\rightarrow V$ is linear, then
>$$[T^*]_\beta^\beta=\left([T]_\beta^\beta\right)^*$$
>(conjugate transpose)

>[!theorem] Properties of the Adjoint
>Let $V$ be a finite-dimensional inner product space. and let $U,T:V\rightarrow V$ be linear (linear operators). Then
>1. $(U+T)^*=U^*+T^*$
>2. $(cT)^*=\overline{c}T^*$ for all $c\in F$
>3. $(TU)^*=U^*T^*$
>4. $T^{**}=T$
>5. $\text{Id}^*=\text{Id}$
>$\quad$

***Proof:***
1. $\langle(T+U)(x),y\rangle=\langle x,(T+U)^*(y)\rangle=\langle T(x)+U(x),y\rangle=\langle T(x),y\rangle +\langle U(x),y\rangle$ $=\langle x,T^*(y)\rangle+\langle x,U^*(y)\rangle=\langle x,T^*(y)+U^*(y)\rangle$
2. $\langle x,(cT)^*(y)\rangle=\langle (cT)(x),y\rangle=c\langle T(x),y\rangle=c(x,T^*(y)\rangle=\langle x,\overline{c}T^*(y)\rangle\implies (cT)^*=\overline{c}T^*$
3. $\langle x,(TU)^*(y)\rangle=\langle T(U(x)),y\rangle=\langle U(x),T^*(y)\rangle=\langle x,U^*T^*(y)\rangle\implies (TU^*(y)=U^*T^*(y)$
