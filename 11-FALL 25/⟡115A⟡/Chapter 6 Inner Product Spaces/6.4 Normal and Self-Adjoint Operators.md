>[!theorem] Lemma
>Let $V$ be finite-dimensional inner product space, $T:V\rightarrow V$ linear.
>If $T$ has an eigenvector with eigenvalue $\lambda$, then $T^*$ has an eigenvector with eigenvalue $\overline{\lambda}$.

***Proof:*** Suppose $v$ is an eigenvector corresponding to eigenvalue $\lambda$. Then 
$$(T-\lambda I)(v)=\vec c$$
$$\begin{align}0=\langle 0,x\rangle&=\langle (T-\lambda I)(v),x\rangle\\&=\langle v,(T-\lambda I)^*(x)\rangle\\&=\langle v,(T^*-\overline{\lambda}I)(x)\rangle=0\end{align}$$
which is true for all $x$. So $v\perp\text{Im}(T-\overline{\lambda}I)$, or $v\in\text{Im}(T-\overline{\lambda}I)^\perp$, $v\neq\vec0$ as an eigenvector. So $T^*\overline{\lambda}I$ is not surjective since there is a non-zero vector in its orthogonal complement, and thus also not injective, meaning there is a non-zero vector in its kernel. Thus there are eigenvectors of $T^*$ with eigenvalues $\overline{\lambda}$.

If $V$ is a finite-dimensional inner product space and there is an orthonormal basis $\beta$ of eigenectors of $T$ for $V$, then
$$[T]_\beta\text{ is diagonal }\implies [T^*]_\beta=([T]_\beta)^*\text{ is also diagonal}$$
Diagonal matrices commute, so $$[T]_\beta[T^*]_\beta=[T^*]_\beta[T]_\beta\implies TT^*=T^*T$$
So if $T:V\rightarrow V$ linear and $V$ has orthonormal basis of eigenvectors of $T$, then $T$ is normal. The converse is false.

>[!theorem] Spectral Theorem
>Let $V$ be a finite-dimensional complex $(F=\mathbb{C})$ inner product space and $T:V\rightarrow V$ linear. $T$ is normal if and only if there is an orthonormal basis for $V$ consisting of eigenvectors of $T$.

>[!definition]
>Let $V$ be an inner product space and $T:V\rightarrow V$ be linear. $T$ is **self-adjoint** if $T=T^*$.
>****
>$A\in M_{n\times n}(F)$ is **self-adjoint** if $A=A^*$
>If $F=\mathbb{R}$, $A^*=A^T$, so self-adjoint condition becomes $A=A^T$ (symmetric)

>[!theorem]
>If $\beta$ is an orthonormal basis, then $T$ is self-adjoint if and only if $[T]_\beta^\beta$ is self-adjoint.

Observe that if $D\in M_{n\times n}(\mathbb{C})$ is a diagonal $n\times n$ matrix, then $D$ is self-adjoint if and only if each diagonal entry is real.

>[!theorem]
>Let $T:V\rightarrow V$ be self-adjoint. Then every eigenvalue of $T$ is real. If $F=\mathbb{C}$, eigenvalues are of the form $a+0i=a$.

***Proof:***  Let $T:V\rightarrow V$ be self-adjoint and since $T$ is self adjoint, $T$ is normal. Suppose $T(x)=\lambda x$ for some $\lambda\in\mathbb{C}$. So $T^*(x)=\overline\lambda x$, $\lambda x=T(x)=T^*(x)=\overline\lambda x$. So $\lambda x = \overline\lambda x\implies \lambda=\overline\lambda$. Therefore $\lambda$ is a real number.

>[!theorem]
>Let $T:V\rightarrow V$ be self adjoint, with $V$ being a finite-dimensional inner product space. Then the characteristic polynomial of $T$, $\chi_T(t)$ splits.

***Proof:*** $\chi_T(t)$ splits over $\mathbb{C}$ by the fundamental theorem of algebra. So $\chi_T(t)=\pm(t-\lambda_1)\dots(t-\lambda_n)$. 
But each eigenvalue is real, $\lambda_i\in\mathbb{R}$, by the previous theorem. So $\chi_T(t)$ splits over $\mathbb{R}$ as well.

>[!theorem]
>Let $V$ be a finite-dimensional inner product space over $\mathbb{R}$ and $T:V\rightarrow V$ linear. Then $T$ is self-adjoint if and only if there is an orthonormal basis for $V$ consisting of eigenvectors of $T$.
>In general,
>$$T\text{ self adjoint}\implies\text{orthonormal basis of eigenvectors}$$
>****
>If $V$ is complex instead, and $T:V\rightarrow V$ self-adjoint, then
>$$T\text{ is normal}\implies\text{orthonormal basis of eigenvectors}$$

***Proof:*** $T$ self adjoint $\implies$ $\chi_T(t)$ splits. So by Schur's theorem, there exists an orthonormal basis $\beta$ such that $A=[T]_\beta$ is upper triangular. But $A=[T]_\beta=[T^*]_\beta=([T]_\beta)^*=A^*$, so $A$ and $A^*$ both are upper triangular. Thus $A$ is diagonal. Hence $\beta$ consists of eigenvectors.

*Note:* $T$ self-adjoint $\implies$ eigenvalues real, but eigenvectors can still be complex.
