>[!definition]
>If $z=a+bi$, the **complex conjugate** is defined $$\overline{z}=a-bi$$
>Also
>$$\overline{ab}=\overline{a}\overline{b}$$
>$$\overline{a+b}=\overline{a}+\overline{b}$$
>$$\overline{\overline{a}}=a$$

>[!definition]
>Let $V$ be a vector space over $F$ (where $F=\mathbb{R}$ or $\mathbb{C}$). An **inner product** on $V$ is a function
>$$\langle -,-\rangle :V\times V\rightarrow F$$
>that takes two vectors $v,w\in V$ and produces a scalar $\langle v,w\rangle\in F$. 
>For $a,b\in F$ and $x,y,z\in V$, it satisfies the following axioms:
>**(1-2)** Linear in the first argument $$\langle ax+by,z\rangle=a\langle x,z\rangle+b\langle y,z\rangle$$
>or, $\langle x+z,y\rangle=\langle x,y\rangle+\langle z,y\rangle$ and $\langle cx, y\rangle=c\langle x,y\rangle$
>**(3)** $\langle y,x\rangle = \overline{\langle x,y\rangle}$, or $\langle y,x\rangle=\langle x,y\rangle$ if $F=\mathbb{R}$
>**(4)** If $x\neq\vec0$, then $\langle x,x\rangle$ is a positive real number.
>****
>To prove an inner product we just need to show these (three) properties.

The inner product is linear in the first argument and conjugate linear in the second. Over reals, it is linear in the second.

>[!definition]
>For $$\vec x=\begin{pmatrix}a_1\\\vdots\\a_n\end{pmatrix}\qquad y=\begin{pmatrix}b_1\\\vdots\\b_n\end{pmatrix}\in F^n$$
>then we can define an inner product by 
>$$\langle x,y\rangle=a_1\overline{b_1}+a_2\overline{b_2}+\dots +a_n\overline{b_n}=\sum_{i=1}^n a_i\overline{b_i}$$
>This inner product is called the **standard inner product on $F^n$**. When $F=\mathbb{R}$, this is the **dot product**.

 If $\langle\cdot,\cdot\rangle$ is an inner product on a vector space $V$, and $r>0$ is a positive real number, then $\langle x,y\rangle'=r\langle x,y\rangle$ is also an inner product.

>[!definition]
>Let $A\in M_{m\times n}(F)$. The **conjugate transpose (adjoint)** of $A$ is the $n\times m$ matrix $A^*$ with $$(A^*)_{ij}=\overline{A_{ji}}$$
>(the complex conjugate of the $ji$-th element of $A$)
>$$A\xrightarrow{\text{transpose}}A^T\xrightarrow{\text{conjugate of each entry}}A^*$$
>*Note*: if $F=\mathbb{R}$, then $A^*=A^T$

>[!definition]
>An **inner product space** is a vector space $V$ equipped with an inner product $\langle -,-\rangle$. 
>- It can be real ($F=\mathbb{R}$) or complex ($F=\mathbb{C}$).
>- Then a subspace $W$ of the inner product space $V$ is also an inner product space when the same function $\langle x,y\rangle$ is restricted to $x,y\in W$.
>- Note that two distinct inner products on a given vector space yield two distinct inner product spaces.
>$\quad$

>[!theorem]
>Let $V$ be an inner product space. Then for all vectors $x,y,z\in V$ and scalars $c\in F$, the following properties hold:
>a) $\langle x,y+z\rangle=\langle x,y\rangle + \langle x,z\rangle$
>b) $\langle x,cy\rangle=\overline{c}\langle x,y\rangle$
>c) $\langle x,\vec0\rangle=0=\langle \vec0,x\rangle$
>d) $\langle x,x\rangle=0\iff x=\vec0$
>e) If $\langle x,y\rangle=\langle x,z\rangle$ for all $x\in V$, then $y=z$

>[!definition]
>Let $V$ be an inner product space. For $x\in V$, the **norm** of $x$ is
>$$||x||=\sqrt{\langle x,x\rangle}$$ aka *length*, *magnitude*
>****
>For $\mathbb{R}^2$ with the standard inner product,
>$$\lVert \begin{pmatrix}a\\b\end{pmatrix}\rVert=\sqrt{a^2+b^2}$$
>For $\mathbb{C}^2$ with standard inner product,
>$$\lVert \begin{pmatrix}a\\b\end{pmatrix}\rVert=\sqrt{|a|^2+|b|^2}$$

>[!theorem]
>Let $V$ be an inner product space over $F$. Then for all $x,y\in V$ and $c\in F$
>a) $\lVert cx\rVert = |c| \lVert x\rVert$
>b) $\lVert x\rVert = 0$ if and only if $x=\vec0$. Also, $\lVert x\rVert \geq 0$
>c) *Cauchy-Schwarz inequality*
>$$|\langle x,y\rangle |\leq \lVert x\rVert \cdot \lVert y\rVert$$
>d) *Triangle inequality*
>$$\lVert x+y\rVert\leq\lVert x\rVert + \lVert y\rVert$$

***Proof (a):***
$\lVert cx\rVert=\sqrt{\langle cx,cx\rangle}=\sqrt{c\overline{c}\langle x,x\rangle}=\sqrt{|c|^2\langle x,x\rangle}=|c|\sqrt{\langle x,x\rangle}=|c|\lVert x\rVert$

***Proof (c):***
Obvious if $y=0$.
$c=\dfrac{\langle x,y\rangle}{\lVert y\rVert ^2}$
$0\leq\lVert x-cy\rVert^2=\langle x-cy,x-cy\rangle$
$=\langle x,x-cy\rangle -c\langle y,x-cy\rangle$
$=\langle x,x\rangle - \langle x,cy\rangle -c(\langle y,x\rangle - \langle y,cy\rangle )$
$=\lVert x\rVert^2-\overline{c}\langle x,y\rangle - c\langle y,x\rangle +  c\overline{c}\lVert y\rVert^2$
$=\| x\|^2-\overline{c}\langle x,y\rangle -c\langle y,x\rangle + |c|^2\|y\|^2$
Because 
$$\overline{c}\langle x,y\rangle=\dfrac{\overline{\langle x,y\rangle}\langle x,y\rangle}{\|y\|^2},\qquad c\langle y,x\rangle = \dfrac{\langle x,y\rangle\langle y,x\rangle}{\| y \|^2},\qquad|c|^2\|y\|^2=\dfrac{\langle x,y\rangle}{\|y\|^2}\dfrac{\overline{\langle x,y\rangle}}{\|y\|^2}\|y\|^2=\dfrac{\langle x,y\rangle \overline{\langle x,y\rangle}}{\|y\|^2}$$
then these three expressions are all equal to $\dfrac{|\langle x,y\rangle |^2}{\|y\|^2}$
$=\|x\|^2-\dfrac{|\langle x,y\rangle |^2}{\|y\|^2}(-1-1+1)$
$=\|x\|^2-\dfrac{|\langle x,y\rangle |^2}{\|y\|^2}\geq 0$
$\implies |\langle x,y\rangle |^2\leq (\|x\|\|y\|)^2$

***Proof (d):***
$\|x+y\|^2=\langle x+y,x+y\rangle$
$=\langle x,x+y\rangle +\langle y,x+y\rangle$
$=\langle x,x\rangle + \langle x,y\rangle + \langle y,x\rangle + \langle y,y\rangle$
$=\|x\|^2+\|y\|^2+\langle x,y\rangle + \langle y,x\rangle$
Because $\langle x,y\rangle + \langle y,x\rangle=2\text{Re}(\langle x,y\rangle)$ (the real part) then by the *Cauchy-Schwarz Inequality (c)*
$\|x\|^2+\|y\|^2+2\text{Re}(\langle x,y\rangle)\leq \|x\|^2+\|y\|^2+2\|x\|\|y\|=(\|x\|+\|y\|)^2$
$\|x+y\|^2\leq(\|x\|+\|y\|)^2$
$\implies \|x+y\|\leq \|x\|+\|y\|$

>[!definition]
>Let $V$ be an inner product space. A **unit vector** is a vector $x\in V$ with $$\|x\|=1$$

>[!definition]
>For a non-zero vector $x\in V$, the **normalization** of $x$ is $$\dfrac{x}{\|x\|}$$

>[!definition]
>Let $V$ be an inner product space. A pair of vectors $x$ and $y$ in $V$ are **orthogonal** if $$\langle x,y\rangle =0$$

>[!definition]
>Let $V$ be an inner product space. A subset $S\subseteq V$ is an **orthonormal subset** if it is *orthogonal* (any pair of distinct vectors is orthogonal) and every vector is a *unit vector*.
>An orthonormal subset will never contain the zero vector.
