>[!definition]
>Suppose $x$ is a discrete random variable (DRV) with $S_x$, $\text{pmf}f(x)$. Let $u(x)$ be a function, then if the sum
>$$\sum_{x\in S_x}u(x)f(x)$$ exists (is finite) then the sum is a **mathematical expectation** (expected value) of $u(x)$ and its notation $$E[u(X)]=\sum_{x\in S_x}u(x)f(x)$$

>[!theorem]
>When it exists, the expectation $E$ states:
>1. If $c$ is constant, $E[c]=C$.
>2. If $c\in \mathbb{R}$ and $u(X)$ is a function, then
>$$E[c,u(X)]=cE[u(x)]$$
>3. If $c_1,c_2$ are constant and $u_1(X)$ and $u_2(X)$ are functions,
>$$E[c_1u_1(X)+c_2u_2(X)]=c_1E[u_1(X)]+c_2E[u_2(X)]$$

## Special expectations

>[!definition]
>For a random variable $X$:
>(i) the **mean** is denoted $\mu:=E[X]$
>(ii) the **variance** is denoted $\sigma^2=E((X-\mu)^2)$
>(iii) the **standard deviation** is denoted $\sigma:=\sqrt{\sigma^2}$

**Interpretation of the variance:**
$c\in \mathbb{R}; g(c)=E[(X-c)^2]$ for a discrete RV. 
Then $E[X^2-2Xc+c^2]=E[x^2]-E[2Xc]+E[c^2]$
$=E[x^2]-2cE[x]+c^2$
$=c^2-2cE[X]+E[X^2]$
$g(c)=c^2-2c\mu +E[X^2]=c^2-2c^2+E[X^2]=E[X^2]-\mu^2$

## Moment generating functions
**Ex.**
A probability experiment has probability of success $p\in (0, 1)$ and probability of failure $q=1-p$. This experiment is repeated independently until the first success occurs. Say it happens on trial $X$: $x\in S_X=\{1,2,3,\dots,\}$. What is $P(X=x)$ if $x\in S_X$?
**Analysis:**
$P(X=x)\implies (x-1) \text{ failures }, 1\text{ success}$
Because events are independent, 
$$f(x)=P(X=x)=q^{(x-1)}p$$
$$\sum_{x\in S_X}P(X=x)=\sum_{x\in S_x}p\cdot q^{x-1}$$
$\mu=\sum_{x=1}^\infty=f(1)+2f(2)+3f(3)+\dots=p+2pq+3pq^2+4pq^3+\dots$
$q\mu=q\sum^\infty_{x=1}f(x)=pq+2pq^2+3pq^3+4pq^4+\dots$
$\mu-q\mu=\mu(1-q)=p+pq+pq^2+pq^3+pq^4+\dots$
$\mu(1-q)=p(1+q+q^2+q^3+\dots)=p(\dfrac{1}{1-q})=\dfrac{p}{p}=1$
$\mu(1-q)=1\implies\mu=\dfrac{1}{1-q}=\dfrac{1}{p}$

>[!definition]
>Given a discrete random variable (DRV) $X$ and its pmf $f(x)$;
>if there exists a positive constant $h$ such that for $t\in(-h,h)$, the following expectation function $$E[e^{tX}]=\sum_{x\in S_X}e^{tx}f(x)$$ exists and it is called the **moment generating function (MGF)$:\equiv M_X(t)$**.
>Also can write it as $$M_X(t)=\sum P(X=x)e^{tx}$$

**Ex.** Suppose $X$ with pmf

| $x$    | -2  | 0   | 1   |
| ------ | --- | --- | --- |
| $f(x)$ | 1/2 | 1/3 | 1/6 |
$E[e^{tx}]=M_X(t)\sum_{x\in S_X}e^{tx}f(x)=e^{-2t}(\dfrac{1}{2})+e^0(\dfrac{1}{3})+e^t(\dfrac{1}{6})=\dfrac{e^{-2t}}{2}+\dfrac{1}{3}+\dfrac{e^t}{6}$

>[!theorem]
>The MGF determines a random variable $X$; i.e. if $X$ and $Y$ are two $V$ such that $$PM_X(t)=M_Y(t)\forall t\in(-h,h)$$ then $S_X=S_Y$ and $f_X(x)=f_Y(x)$ for $x\in S_x=S_y$.

$$E[e^{tx}]=\sum_{x\in S_x}e^{tx}f(x)=M_X(t)$$
**Ex.** $X$ has a geometric distribution with parameter $p\in[0,1]$ (notation: $X$~$\text{Geom}(p)$).
$\text{pmf}f(k)=P(X=k)=q^{k-1}p$, with $q=1-p$, $k=1,2,3,\dots$
Then $$M_X(t)=E[e^{tx}]=\sum^\infty_{k=1}e^{tk}f(k)=\sum_{k=1}^\infty e^{tk}(q^{k-1}p)$$
$=e^tp+e^{2t}qp+e^{3t}pq^2+\dots$

$=pe^t(1+e^tq+(e^tq)^2+(e^tq)^3+\dots+)$
$$=\dfrac{pe^t}{1-e^tq}\text{ with }0<e^tq<1$$
Then $t<\ln(\frac{1}{q})$:
$$M_X(t)\equiv E[e^{tx}]=\dfrac{pe^t}{1-e^tq},\hspace{2em}t<\ln(\dfrac{1}{1-p})$$
>[!definition]
>$n\in\mathbb{N}=\{0,1,2,3,\dots\}$,
>$$E[X^n]=\sum_{x\in S_x}x^nf(x)<\infty$$ $\implies E[X^n]$ is called the **nth (order) moment**.

>[!definition] Properties of mgf $M_X(t)$
>1. $$M_X(t=0)=E[e^0x]=E[1]=1$$
>2. Derivatives:
>$$\frac{d}{dt}(M_X(t))=\frac{d}{dt}E[e^{tx}]=E[\frac{d}{dt}e^{tx}]=E[xe^{tx}]$$
>$$\frac{d}{dt}(\frac{d}{dt}M_X(t))=E[x^2e^{tx}]$$
>$M_X'(t=0)=E[x]$, $M_X''(t=0)=E[x^2]$
>$\quad$

$\sigma^2=E[(X-\mu)^2]=E[X^2-2X\mu+\mu^2]$
$=E[X^2]-E[2X\mu]+E[\mu^2]$
$=E[X^2]-2\mu\cdot E[X]+\mu^2$
$=E[X^2]-2(E[X])^2+(E[X])^2$

$\sigma^2=M_X''(t=0)-(M'_x(t=0))^2$
We can generalize: $M_X^{(n)}(t)=E[X^n]$

For $X$~$\text{Geom}(p)$:
$M_X(t)=\dfrac{pe^t}{1-qe^t},\hspace{1em}q=1-p$
$M_X'(t)=\dfrac{pe^t(1-qe^t)+qe^tpe^t}{(1-qe^t)^2}=\dfrac{pe^t}{(1-qe^t)^2}$
$\implies M_X'(t=0)=\dfrac{p}{(1-q)^2}=\dfrac{1}{p}=E[X]$

### Notes
For simplifying the MGF sum, write it in the form
$$\sum_{n=k}^\infty r^n=\dfrac{r^k}{1-r}$$
