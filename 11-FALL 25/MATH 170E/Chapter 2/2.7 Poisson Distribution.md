### Three conditions of Poisson processes
**Assumption (1)** Consider the arrival of guests in a *continuous* time interval. These arrivals in non-overlapping subintervals are *independent*.
**Assumption (2)** There exists a fixed $\lambda>0$ such that for a sufficiently short subinterval of time $\epsilon>0$, 
$$P(\text{exactly one guest arrives in }[a,a+\epsilon])=\lambda\epsilon$$
**Assumption (3)** The probability of two or more arrivals in the same subinterval is essentially zero:
$$P(\text{at least two guests arrived in }[a,a+\epsilon])=0$$
Then,
$$\implies P(\text{no arrival in }[a,a+\epsilon])=1-\lambda\epsilon$$
Assume that $X$ is a random variable counting the number of arrivals in one hour. 

What is $P(X=k)$?

***Solution*:** Partition the time interval into $n$ subunits of equal length $\dfrac{1}{n}$. $n$ is large $(n\rightarrow\infty)$, so $\dfrac{1}{n}$ is very small. By Assumption (2),
$$P(\text{1 arrival in 1 subinterval})=\lambda\cdot\dfrac{1}{n}=\dfrac{\lambda}{n}$$
By Assumption (1), the arrival in each subinterval is independent. Then
$$P(X=k)=P(k\text{ subintervals having 1 arrival each among $n$ subintervals})$$
$$P(X=k)=\dbinom{n}{k}\left(\dfrac{\lambda}{n}\right)^k\left(1-\dfrac{\lambda}{n}\right)^{n-k}$$
As $n\rightarrow\infty$, 
$$\lim_{n\rightarrow\infty}\dfrac{n!}{x!(n-x)!}\left(\dfrac{\lambda}{n}\right)^k\left(1-\dfrac{\lambda}{n}\right)^{n-k}$$
$$=\lim_{n\rightarrow\infty}\dfrac{n(n-1)(n-2)\dots(n-k+1)}{n\times n\times \dots\times n=n^k}\cdot\dfrac{\lambda^k}{k!}\left(1-\dfrac{\lambda}{n}\right)^n\left(1-\dfrac{\lambda}{n}\right)^{-k}$$
We have 
$$\lim_{n\rightarrow\infty}\dfrac{n(n-1)(n-2)\dots(n-k+1)}{n^k}=1\hspace{2em}\lim_{n\rightarrow\infty}\left(1-\dfrac{\lambda}{n}\right)^n=1\hspace{2em}\lim_{n\rightarrow\infty}\left(1-\dfrac{\lambda}{n}\right)^{-k}=1$$
Thus:
$$\boxed{P(X=k)=\dfrac{\lambda^k}{k!}e^{-\lambda}}$$
>[!definition]
>Let $X$ be a DRV taking values $\{0,1,2,\dots\}$ with *pmf* $P(X=k)=\dfrac{\lambda^k}{k!}e^{-\lambda}$ for a fixed $\lambda>0$. Then $X$ is called **Poisson distribution** and is denoted
>$$X\sim\text{Pois}(x)$$

**Additional interpretation**
Poisson distribution is a discrete PD that models the number of events which occur in fixed intervals of time or space.
1. Independent of each other
2. At a constant average rate $\lambda$
3. The probability of at least two events occurring in an infinitesimal small interval is 0.

### Properties
$X\sim\text{Poisson}(\lambda), P(X=k)=\dfrac{\lambda^k}{k!}e^{-\lambda}$
Check that the pmf sums to 1:
$$\sum^\infty_{k=0}P(X=k)=\sum^\infty_{k=0}e^{-\lambda}\cdot\dfrac{\lambda^k}{k!}=e^{-\lambda}\cdot\sum^\infty_{k=0}\dfrac{\lambda^k}{k!}=e^{-\lambda}(e^\lambda)=1$$
#### Moment generating function
$$M_X(t)=E(e^{tx})=\sum^\infty_{k=0}e^{tk}f(k)=\sum^\infty_{k=0}e^{tk}e^{-\lambda}\cdot\dfrac{\lambda^k}{k!}$$
$$=\sum^\infty_{k\geq 0}e^{tk}e^{-\lambda}\cdot\dfrac{\lambda^k}{k!}=\sum^\infty_{k\geq 0}e^{-\lambda}\cdot\dfrac{(e^t\lambda)^k}{k!}=e^{-\lambda}e^{e^t\lambda}=e^{\lambda(e^t-1)}$$
$$\implies \boxed{M_X(t)=e^{\lambda(e^t-1)}}$$
#### Mean
$$E(X)=M'_X(t=0)=\lambda e^te^{\lambda(e^t-1)}=\lambda$$
$$\boxed{E(X)=\lambda}$$
#### Variance
$$M'_X(t=0)=\lambda(e^te^{\lambda(e^t-1)}+e^t\lambda e^0e^{\lambda(e^0-1)})=\lambda(+\lambda)=\lambda+\lambda^2$$
$$\sigma^2=M_X''(t=0)-(M'_X(t=0))^2=\lambda+\lambda^2-\lambda^2=\lambda$$
$$\boxed{\sigma^2=\lambda}$$
### Using the Poisson distribution to approximate binomial distribution
For $X\sim \text{binom}(n,p)$ with large $n$ and small $p$:
$$\dfrac{(np^x)e^{-np}}{x!}\approx \dbinom{n}{x}p^x(1-p)^{n-x}$$
