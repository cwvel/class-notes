
### Correlation Coefficients
>[!definition]
>The **covariance**, denoted $\text{Cov}=\sigma_{xy}$ is given by
>$$\text{Cov}(x,y)=\sigma_{XY}=E((X-\mu_X)(Y-\mu_Y))$$

>[!definition]
>The **correlation coefficient**, denoted $\rho$, is given by
>$$\rho=\dfrac{\sigma_{XY}}{\sigma_X\sigma_Y}=\dfrac{\text{Cov}(X,Y)}{\sigma_X\sigma_Y}$$

>[!theorem]
>1. The covariance $\sigma_{XY}=\text{Cov}(X,Y)=E(XY)-E(X)E(Y)=E(XY)-\mu_X\mu_Y$
>2. If $X$ and $Y$ are independent, then
>$$E(u(X)u(Y))=E(u(X))E(u(Y))$$
>for any $u(X)$ and $u(Y)$, and $$\sigma_{XY}=0$$
>***But*** $\sigma_{XY}=0$ does not imply $X$ and $Y$ are independent.
>$\quad$

**Ex.** $X$ and $Y$ are two random variables defined as
$$X=1,2,\qquad  P(X=1)=1/2, P(X=2)=1/2,\qquad Y=3X$$
Then the expected values are:
$$E(X)=\sum_{S_X}xP(X=x)=1\cdot P(X=1)+2P(X=2)=1\cdot\dfrac{1}{2}+2\cdot\dfrac{1}{2}=\dfrac{3}{2}$$
$$E(Y)=E(3X)=3E(X)=3\left(\dfrac{3}{2}\right)=\dfrac{9}{2}$$
To find $E(XY)$:
$$E(XY)=E(X\cdot 3X)=E(3X^2)=3E(X^2)$$
Find $E(X^2)$:
$$E(X^2)=\sum_{S_X}x^2P(X=x)=\dfrac{5}{2}$$
then
$$E(XY)=3\cdot\dfrac{5}{2}=\dfrac{15}{2}$$
Find the covariance:
$$\text{Cov}(X,Y)=E((X-E(X))(Y-E(Y)))=E(XY)-E(X)E(Y)=\dfrac{3}{4}$$
Find the variances:
$$\sigma^2(X)=E(X^2)-(E(X))^2=\left(\dfrac{5}{2}\right)-\left(\dfrac{3}{2}\right)^2=\dfrac{1}{4},\qquad\sigma=\dfrac{1}{2}$$
$$\sigma^2(Y)=E(Y)-(E(Y))^2=E(9X^2)-\left(\dfrac{9}{2}\right)^2=\dfrac{9}{4},\qquad \sigma=\dfrac{3}{2}$$
Then
$$\rho(X,Y)=\dfrac{\text{Cov}(X,Y)}{\sigma_X\sigma_Y}=\dfrac{\frac{3}{4}}{\frac{1}{2}\cdot\frac{3}{2}}=1$$

**Ex.** Counter-example

| X   | Y   | P   | XY  |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| -1  | 1   | 1/3 | -1  |
| 0   | 0   | 1/3 | 0   |
| 1   | 1   | 1/3 | 1   |
$Y$ is depending on $X$:
$\text{Cov}(X,Y)=E(XY)-E(X)E(Y)$
$E(X)=0$, $E(Y)=2/3$, $E(XY)=0$
Then $\text{Cov}(X,Y)=0-0=0$ but $X$ and $Y$ are not independent.
