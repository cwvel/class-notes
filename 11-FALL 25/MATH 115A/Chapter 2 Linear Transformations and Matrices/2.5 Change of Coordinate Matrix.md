Recall: If $\beta=\{v_1\dots v_n\}$ is an ordered basis for the $n$-dimensional vector space $V$, then $V\rightarrow F^n$ is an isomorphism: $V\mapsto [V]_\beta$

What if we choose a different basis $\beta'$?

For $x\in V$, $x=\text{Id}_V(x)$
>[!theorem]
>Let $\beta$ and $\beta'$ be ordered bases for $V$.
>a) For $x\in V$, $$[x]_\beta=[\text{Id}_V]^\beta_{\beta'}[x]_{\beta'}$$
>b) The matrix $Q=[\text{Id}_V]^\beta_{\beta'}$ is invertible with inverse $$Q^{-1}=[\text{Id}_V]^{\beta'}_\beta$$

***Proof:*** (a) is proven from the theorem $[T(x)]_\gamma=[T]^\gamma_\beta[x]_\beta$, (b) from $[T^{-1}]^\gamma_\beta=([T]^\beta_\gamma)^{-1}$.

>[!definition]
>Let $\beta$ and $\beta'$ be ordered bases for $V$. Then $$Q=[\text{Id}_V]^\beta_{\beta'}$$ is the **change of coordinate matrix** from $\beta'$ to $\beta$ ($\text{id}:V\rightarrow V$) and it is invertible:
>$$[v]_\beta=[\text{id}]^\beta_{\beta'}[v]_{\beta'}]$$
>$$[T]_{\beta'}=([\text{id}]^\beta_{\beta'})^{-1}[T]_\beta[\text{id}]^\beta_{\beta'}$$
>$$[T]_{\beta'}=Q^{-1}[T]_\beta Q$$
>

>[!theorem] General theorem
>Let $T:V\rightarrow W$ be linear, $\beta,\beta'$ ordered bases for $V$ and $\gamma,\gamma'$ ordered bases for $W$. Then
>$$[T]^{\gamma'}_{\beta'}=[\text{Id}]^{\gamma'}_\gamma[T]^\gamma_\beta[\text{Id}]^\beta_{\beta'}$$
>Case of interest: $V=W$, $\beta=\gamma$, $\beta'=\gamma'$. Then $T:V\rightarrow V$ linear and $\beta, \beta'$ ordered bases of $V$. Let $Q=[\text{Id}]^\beta_{\beta'}$, then
>$$[T]^\beta_{\beta'}=[T]_\beta=Q[T]_{\beta'}Q^{-1}$$

>[!definition]
>Two matrices $A,B\in M_{n\times n}(F)$ are **similar** if there exists an invertible matrix $Q$ such that $A=QBQ^{-1}$.
