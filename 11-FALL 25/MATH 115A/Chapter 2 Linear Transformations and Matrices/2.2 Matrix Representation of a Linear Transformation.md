>[!definition]
>An **ordered basis** for a finite-dimensional vector space $V$ is a basis endowed with a specific order.

>[!definition]
>Let $\beta=\{v_1,\dots,v_n\}$ be an ordered basis for a finite-dimensional vector space $V$.
>Then for $x\in V, x=c_1v_1+c_2v_2+\dots+c_nv_n$ for some $c_i\in F$.
>The **coordinate vector of $x$ relative to $\beta$** is
>$$[x]_\beta = \begin{pmatrix}c_1\\c_2\\ \vdots \\c_n\end{pmatrix}\in F^n$$

$$\boxed{\begin{matrix}
& {T} \\
V & \longrightarrow & W \\
[\quad]_\beta \downarrow/ \uparrow [\quad]_\beta^{-1} & & [\quad]_\gamma^{-1}\downarrow/\uparrow [\quad]_\gamma \\
F^n & \longrightarrow & F^m \\
& ?=[T]_\beta^\gamma
\end{matrix}}$$
**Chase the standard basis vector around the diagram:**
$e_i$ is the coordinate vector of $v_i\in \beta$. 
($[\quad]_\beta^{-1}$ is the transformation given by $\begin{pmatrix}c_1\\\vdots\\c_n\end{pmatrix}\mapsto c_1v_1+\dots+c_nv_n$)
$\rightarrow[\quad]^{-1}_\beta(e_i)=v_i$
Next, $T(v_i)$.
Finally, take the coordinates of $T(v_i)$ with respect to $\gamma$ to get $[T(v_i)]_\gamma$.

>[!definition] 
>Let $T:V\rightarrow W$ be linear and let $\beta=\{v_1,\dots,v_n\}$ and $\gamma=\{w_1,\dots,w_m\}$ be ordered bases for $V$ and $W$ respectively. The **matrix representation** of $T$ with respect to $\beta$ and $\gamma$ is the matrix
>$$[T]^\gamma_\beta=(a_{ij})\text{ for }T(v_j)=\sum_{i=1}^ma_{ij}w_j$$
>or
>$$[T]^\gamma_\beta=([T(v_1)]_\gamma \hspace{1em}[T(v_2)]_\gamma\hspace{1em}\dots\hspace{1em}[T(v_n)]_\gamma)$$
>where the columns of the matrix are basis vectors. 
>****
>Plug in each basis vector of $\beta$ into $T$ (compute $T(v_j)$), then write them in the basis of $\gamma$. These vectors form the columns of the matrix.
>*Note*: In the special case when $V=W$ and $\beta=\gamma$, then we're mapping a vector space to itself. Then $[T]_\beta$ can denote $[T]^\beta_\beta$.
>$[T]^\gamma_\beta$ has dimensions $\dim W$ by $\dim V$

>[!definition]
>Let $V$ and $W$ be vector spaces in $F$.
>$\mathcal{L}(V,W)=\{\text{the set of all linear transformations }\hspace{0.5em}V\rightarrow W\}$
>If $V=W$, then we write $\mathcal{L}(V)$.

>[!definition]
>Given $T,U\in \mathcal{L}(V,W)$, define $T+U:V\rightarrow W$ as the function $$(T+U)(v)=T(v)+U(v)$$
>Given $c\in F$, define $cT:V\rightarrow W$ by $$(cT)(v)=c(T(v))=T(cv)$$

>[!theorem]
>Given $c\in F$ and linear transformations $U,T:V\rightarrow W$
>a) $cT+U$ is linear
>b) $\mathcal{L}(V,W)$ is a vector space

***Proof*:**
$(cT+U)(dv_1+v_2)=\dots=d(cT+U)(v_1)+(cT+U)(v_2)$
The zero transformation plays the role of the zero vector.

>[!theorem]
>Let $V$ and $W$ be finite-dimensional vector spaces with ordered bases $\beta$ and $\gamma$ respectively, and let $T,U:V\rightarrow W$ be linear transformations. Then
>(a) $[T+U]^\gamma_\beta=[T]^\gamma_\beta+[U]^\gamma_\beta$
>(b) $[aT]^\gamma_\beta=a[T]^\gamma_\beta$

*Remark:* $[T]^\gamma_\beta=[U]^\gamma_\beta$ implies $T=U$.
