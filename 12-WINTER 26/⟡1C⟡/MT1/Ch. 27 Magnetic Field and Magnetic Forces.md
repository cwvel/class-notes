# 27.2 Magnetic field
A moving charge/current (with velocity $\vec v$) creates a magnetic field $\vec B$, in addition to its electric field.
$$\left[\frac{N\cdot s}{C\cdot m}\right]=\left[\frac{N}{A\cdot m}\right]=[T]$$
$T$ = Tesla, $10^{-4} T=1\enspace G$ (Gauss)
A magnetic field exerts a force $\vec F$ on other moving charges/currents.
Magnitude of this force is:
$$F=|q|v_\perp B=|q|vB\sin\phi$$
$B\sin\phi$ is the component of $\vec B$ that is perpendicular to $\vec v$.
$F=F_{max}$ when $\phi=90^\circ$, and $F=0$ when $\vec B\parallel\vec v$.
And $\vec F$ is always perpendicular to both $\vec B$ and $\vec v$:
$$\vec F=q\vec v\times \vec B$$
- $\vec F$ = magnetic force on moving charged particle
- $q$ = particle's charge
- $\vec v$ = particle's velocity
- $\vec B$ = magnetic field
![300](../../pasted_images/Pasted%20image%2020260120142110.png)
Both electric and magnetic fields exert a force on a particle:
$$\vec F=q(\vec E+\vec v\times \vec B)$$
![300](../../pasted_images/Pasted%20image%2020260120142200.png)
# 27.3 Magnetic field lines
At each point, the magnetic field line is tangent to $\vec B$ (points in the same direction a compass would, NORTH$\rightarrow$SOUTH)
More densely packed field lines indicate a stronger field.
![300](../../pasted_images/Pasted%20image%2020260120142440.png)
#### Total magnetic flux through a surface:
$$\Phi_B=\int B\cos\phi\enspace dA=\int \vec B\cdot\vec A$$
Total magnetic flux through any closed surface is 0:
$$\oint\vec B\cdot d\vec A=0$$
![200](../../pasted_images/Pasted%20image%2020260120142519.png)
# 27.4 Motion of charged particles
Since $\vec F$ is always perpendicular to $\vec v$, the magnetic field cannot change the *magnitude* of the velocity, only it's *direction*. $\vec B$ never does work on the particle. Thus $\vec F$ and $\vec v$ are always **constant.**
A charged particle moves in a plane **perpendicular** to a uniform $\vec B$, in a circle of radius $R$ with constant speed $v$:
$$F=|q|vB=m\cdot\frac{v^2}{R}$$
From the centripetal force equation we can get:
$$R=\frac{mv}{|q|B}$$
- $R$ = radius of a circular orbit in a magnetic field
- $m$ = particle's mass
- $v$ = particle's speed
- $q$ = particle's charge
- $B$ = magnetic field magnitude
![300](../../pasted_images/Pasted%20image%2020260120142552.png)
**A negative charge moves clockwise around the orbit.**
Also since $v=R\omega$, we can find the particle's angular velocity:
$$\omega=\frac{|q|B}{m}$$
Also $T=\dfrac{2\pi}{\omega}=\dfrac{2\pi m}{qB}$.
If the direction of $\vec v$ is **not perpendicular** to $\vec B$ (and not parallel), $\vec v_\parallel$ is constant, and the particle moves in a **helix**.

# 27.7 Force and torque on a current loop
![Pasted image 20260120144014](../../pasted_images/Pasted%20image%2020260120144014.png)
- Each pair cancels out $\rightarrow$ no net force
- The forces $\vec F$, $-\vec F$ produce a torque on the loop
	- Maximum when $\phi=90^\circ$, zero when $\phi=0^\circ$ (stable) or $180^\circ$ (unstable)

#### Magnitude of magnetic torque on current loop:
$$\tau=IBA\sin\phi=\mu B\sin\phi$$
- $\tau$ = magnitude of magnetic torque
- $I$ = current
- $B$ = magnitude of magnetic field
- $A$ = area of loop
- $\phi$ = angle between normal vector to the loop and $\vec B$

#### Magnetic dipole moment:
$$\vec\mu=I\vec A$$
![200](../../pasted_images/Pasted%20image%2020260120145331.png)
So we also have:
$$\vec \tau=\vec\mu\times\vec B$$
Note: Any planar current loop can be approximated by rectangular loops since all adjacent forces cancel.
![200](../../pasted_images/Pasted%20image%2020260120145602.png)

#### Potential energy for magnetic dipole in magnetic field:
$$U=-\vec\mu\cdot\vec B=-\mu B\cos\phi$$
- $\vec\mu$ = magnetic dipole moment
- $\vec B$ = magnetic field
- $\phi$ = angle between $\vec\mu$ and $\vec B$
