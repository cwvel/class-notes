- Meiosis produces haploid gametes
- When nondisjunction occurs at meiosis I, *all* the gametes produced end up with an incorrect # of chromosomes

- **Gene:** segment of DNA that influences one or more phenotypes
- **Allele:** particular form of a gene
	- Different alleles have different DNA sequences
- **Locus:** stretch of DNA at a particular *location* on a chromosome

 - A **polymorphism** is a nucleotide pair, gene, or region of the genome that has escaped DNA repair mechanisms sometime in the past.
	 - To be considered a polymorphism, the least common variant must occur in at least 1% of the population.
	 - **SNPs** must occur with a certain threshold in the population. They are the most common type of polymorphism

- **Gene duplication**: Duplication of an entire gene due to errors in DNA replication
- **Gene divergence**: A gene gradually accumulates mutations

- **Large chromosomal inversions** are structural rearrangements where a chromosome segment breaks in two places and reinserts in reverse orientation, often acting as "supergenes" that lock beneficial gene combinations together. 
	- These inversions *reduce recombination* in heterozygotes, facilitating rapid adaptation, speciation, and the maintenance of diverse traits within populations
	- Can cause problems during *meiosis* when homologous chromosomes are unable to match up.

- **Recombination frequency**
	- How likely are we to split the two genes apart if we randomly select a position on the chromosome?
	- Maximum is 50%, meaning half the offspring are recombinant.

**Population**: Individuals in same species, time and space
Population will only follow $p^2+2pq+q^2=1$ if in Hardy-Weinberg equilibrium.

Hardy-Weinberg is a model that we compare a population to to test if it is experiencing evolution/in equilibrium.

**Fixation** via **genetic drift**: A specific allele becomes the only variant for a gene in a population (100% frequency) due to random chance (rather than natural selection). 
- This reduces genetic variation is most pronounced in small or bottlenecked populations.
- Does not "favor" advantageous mutations over harmful ones, because it is purely random.

**Genetic divergence**: Isolated populations accumulate independent genetic changes (mutations, drift, selection) over time, increasing differences in their DNA sequences

A population with lower fitness will move faster toward the peak. than a population with higher relative fitness

- **monophyletic**: all members share a single common ancestor that is not shared with any other species or group of species. also called a **clade**
	- contains all the descendants of a common ancestor
- **paraphyletic**: includes some, but not all, of the descendants of a common ancestor
- **polyphyletic**: Groupings that do not include the last common ancestor of all members
	- Members of a polyphyletic group share traits that evolved independently by convergent evolution.
