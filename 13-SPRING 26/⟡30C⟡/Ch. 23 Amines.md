# Chirality, Physical properties
## 23.3 Chirality
2* and 3* amines with three different groups are chiral but usually cannot be resolved because they undergo **pyramidal inversion**, where the nitrogen rapidly oscillates from one side of the plane of the three atoms to the other.
![Pasted image 20260412191603](../pasted_images/Pasted%20image%2020260412191603.png)
![350](../pasted_images/Pasted%20image%2020260413113636.png)
Note that this is not possible for 4* ammonium ions so these salts can be resolved.

## 23.4 Physical properties
Amines are polar.
1* and 2* amines can make intramolecular hydrogen bonds
- Compared to the analogous hydrocarbon, they have higher melting/boiling points, and are more soluble in water
- H-N hydrogen bonds are weaker than H-O hydrogen bonds, so amines have a lower boiling point than the corresponding alcohol

# 23.5 Basicity
Amines are weak bases. In a reaction with water, the equilibrium lies to the left. Ammonium cannot deprotonate water, **it is a weaker base than hydroxide**.
![300](../pasted_images/Pasted%20image%2020260415222849.png)
However, it can deprotonate acid (H3O+) thus **it is a stronger base than H2O**.
![300](../pasted_images/Pasted%20image%2020260415222919.png)
We compare the basicity of amines by referencing the pKa of the conjugate acid (weaker conjugate acid = higher pKa = more basic amine)
![250](../pasted_images/Pasted%20image%2020260415222951.png)
![250](../pasted_images/Pasted%20image%2020260415223014.png)
## Aliphatic amines, pKa ~ 10-11
Aliphatic amines are slightly stronger bases than ammonia.
- Alkyl groups are slightly *electron-releasing* (inductive effect) which stabilizes the conjugate acid
![300](../pasted_images/Pasted%20image%2020260415223311.png)
## Aromatic amines, pka ~4-5
Aniline is much less basic than aliphatic amines for two main reasons.
First, the base is **resonance-stabilized**. The nitrogen lone pair is in resonance with aromatic ring, and this stabilization is lost upon protonation.
![500](../pasted_images/Pasted%20image%2020260415223043.png)
Second, the $sp^2$-hybridized carbons in the ring **inductively withdraws electrons**, decreasing basicity. The nitrogen lone pair is pulled toward the ring and thus less available for protonation.
![250](../pasted_images/Pasted%20image%2020260415224021.png)
****
Electron-releasing substitutents on the ring can increase basicity, while electron-withdrawing substitutents decrease basicity.
**Inductively electron donating.** In this case the lone pair directly next to the ring *donates electrons*, and the negative charge ends up next to the positive charge on the N, stabilizing the conjugate acid.
![250](../pasted_images/Pasted%20image%2020260415223836.png)
![300](../pasted_images/Pasted%20image%2020260415223845.png)
**Inductively electron withdrawing.** Halogens on the ring are electron withdrawing (electronegative atoms).
![200](../pasted_images/Pasted%20image%2020260416110913.png)
**Resonance and induction.**
3-Nitroaniline: Only inductive effect (meta substitution does not experience resonance)
4-Nitroaniline: Both inductive and resonance effects decrease basicity more, electrons can be delocalized onto O
- Same for ortho substitution (2-Nitroananiline)
![250](../pasted_images/Pasted%20image%2020260415224211.png)
![300](../pasted_images/Pasted%20image%2020260415224707.png)

## Heterocyclic aliphatic amines
**Reason: Inductive effect.** Morpholine experiences an electron-withdrawing effect from the O: oxygen pulls the electron density away from the ring, so after protonation the positive charge is less stabilized as in piperidine. Thus morpholine is a weaker base.
![300](../pasted_images/Pasted%20image%2020260415225105.png)
DABCO experiences an electron-withdrawing effect from the extra N, so its conjugate acid is less stabilized than quinuclidine.

## Heterocyclic aromatic amines ~10-11
**Reason: Hybridization.** Because the orbital of the lone pair for the sp3 nitrogen in piperidine is *larger* (more p character), it is more easily protonated. On the other hand the sp2 nitrogen in pyridine has relatively less p character so it is smaller.
![250](../pasted_images/Pasted%20image%2020260415230255.png)

**Reason: Resonance.** The positive charge on the nitrogen in imidazolium can be delocalized across both nitrogens in resonance, stabilizing the conjugate acid. Thus imidazole is a stronger base than pyridine, which is not stabilized by resonance.

# 23.8 Reaction with nitrous acid
Nitrous acid is prepared by reacting NaNO2 with acid.
- Can donate a proton
- Can form the **nitrosyl cation**, a weak electrophile
![Pasted image 20260418174327](../pasted_images/Pasted%20image%2020260418174327.png)

## A. 3* aliphatic amines are *protonated*
Tertiary aliphatic amines are simply protonated to form water-soluble salts.
## B. 3* aromatic amines undergo *nitrosation*
Electrophilic aromatic substitution in rings with ortho-para groups
![Pasted image 20260418174447](../pasted_images/Pasted%20image%2020260418174447.png)
## C. 2* aliphatic/aromatic amines
Nitrosyl cation electrophile + 2* amine nucleophile $\to$ $N$-nitrosamines
![400](../pasted_images/Pasted%20image%2020260418174602.png)
1) **Nucleophilic attack** of amine on nitrosyl cation
2) **Deprotonate** the nitrogen
## D. 1* aliphatic amines
*note:* N=O bond in intermediate should be double bond
![Pasted image 20260418175213](../pasted_images/Pasted%20image%2020260418175213.png)
1) **Nu attack** of N-lp onto nitrosyl cation
2) **Deprotonation** of nitrogen
3) **Keto-enol taut.** gives diazotic acid
4) **Protonate** the diazotic acid OH
5) **Dehydration** of H2O to give *diazonium ion*
6) **Loss of N2** gives carbocation
Produces unstable diazonium salt that loses N2 to give a carbocation. Since the carbocation is very reactive it can lead to mixed products (generally not useful)

### Tiffeneau-Demjanov *ring expansion*
Analogous to pinacol rearrangement.
1) Reaction of 1* amine with nitrous acid gives diazonium ion as above
2) **Loss of N2** gives carbocation but undergoes rearrangement (ring expansion)
3) **Deprotonate** to give ketone
![500](../pasted_images/Pasted%20image%2020260418190117%201.png)

# 23.9 Hofmann elimination
Thermal decomposition of 4* ammonium halide/hydroxide to alkene
- When treated with strong base, a quarternary ammonium halide undergoes $\beta$-elimination by an E2 mechanism to give the less substituted alkene.
![500](../pasted_images/Pasted%20image%2020260419150103.png)
![400](../pasted_images/Pasted%20image%2020260419150109.png)
- Concerted E2 mechanism
- Anti-elimination means H and N must be *antiperiplanar*
- Least hindered H+ getting deprotonated means *less substituted double bond* is formed
