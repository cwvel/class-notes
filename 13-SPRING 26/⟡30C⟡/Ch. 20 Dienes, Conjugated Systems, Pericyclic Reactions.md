## 20.1 Stability of conjugated dienes
### Conjugated systems
Contain two or more adjacent double/triple bonds

**Dienes**: Two C=C bonds
1) **Conjugated diene**: Two double bonds separated by one single bond
2) **Unconjugated diene**: Two double bonds separated by 2+ single bonds
3) **Cumulated diene**: Two double bonds share an $sp$-hybridized carbon (no overlap of $2p$ orbitals thus not conjugated, bonds are perpendicular)
![Pasted image 20260519235039](../pasted_images/Pasted%20image%2020260519235039.png)
### Conjugation (stabilization) energy
![Pasted image 20260529002712](../pasted_images/Pasted%20image%2020260529002712.png)
Two conjugated double bonds are more stable than the analogous unconjugated double bonds
- Due to delocalization of electron density: 4 pi electrons delocalized across 4 parallel p orbitals
	- Instead of each pair of 2 electrons localized between 2 carbons as in 2 unconjugated double bonds
![300](../pasted_images/Pasted%20image%2020260520011438.png)
### $\pi$ molecular models and energy diagrams
- bonding MOs: we *gain* energy by putting electrons in these orbitals
- antibonding MOs: has higher energy than the atomic orbital, won't form bond
![Pasted image 20260601225124](../pasted_images/Pasted%20image%2020260601225124.png)
- a **node** is where the *phase* of the $\pi$ molecular orbital switches

In conjugated systems, the adjacent 2p orbitals overlap in space and combine to produce $\pi$ molecular orbitals that cover all atoms in the conjugated system
- Ground state: All 4 $\pi$ electrons lie in $\pi$-bonding MOs
- Requires that the $2p$ orbitals are parallel (planar geometry required)
![300](../pasted_images/Pasted%20image%2020260520010848.png)

## 20.2 Electrophilic addition to conjugated dienes
Undergo both 1,2- and 1,4-addition reactions with electrophiles, often giving a mixture of these products
- Ratio is temperature dependent
- 1,2-addition dominates at lower temperature
	- it is under **kinetic (rate) control** since usually there is greater positive charge at the 2 position in the cation intermediate, lowering the activation barrier
	- lower temperature prevents equilibration between products, so relative product stability is not important
- 1,4-addition dominates at higher temperature
	- it is under **thermodynamic control** since the resulting double bond is more substituted (lower energy)
	- higher temperature allows equilibration of products so products are distributed based on relative stability
A *Lewis acid* speeds up the reaction
![Pasted image 20260601225259](../pasted_images/Pasted%20image%2020260601225259.png)
### A. 1,2-Addition and 1,4-Addition
1. **Protonation** of one of the double bonds gives resonance-stabilized allylic cation
2. **Nucleophilic attack** from Br of one of the carbons with a positive charge (depending on which one, gives 1,2 or 1,4 product)
![Pasted image 20260601225310](../pasted_images/Pasted%20image%2020260601225310.png)

### B. Kinetic vs. thermodynamic control
Recall Markovnikov's rule: Hydrogens add to the double-bonded carbon with the *greatest* number of hydrogens
![450](../pasted_images/Pasted%20image%2020260520101916.png)
**Kinetic (rate) control:** Distribution of products determined by *relative rates of formation*
- Stronger at lower temperatures since equilibration between reactants and products is usually not happening
- Favors 1,2-addition since the carbon has more of the positive charge, so reaction with bromide occurs more rapidly there
**Thermodynamic (equilibrium) control:** Distribution of products determined by *relative stability*
- Stronger at higher temperatures since reactions become increasingly reversible, establishing equilibrium
- Favors 1,4-addition since it has the more substituted double bond (more stable alkene)
*Note: If the products of low temperature addition are allowed to remain in solution and then are warmed to a higher temperature, the composition of the product changes over time and becomes identical to that obtained when the reaction is carried out at higher temperature*
![400](../pasted_images/Pasted%20image%2020260529012335.png)
![Pasted image 20260520102818](../pasted_images/Pasted%20image%2020260520102818.png)
There are cases where 1,2-addition is more stable and would be the product of thermodynamic control, e.g. here trisubstituted double bond is more stable
![400](../pasted_images/Pasted%20image%2020260601225329.png)
And the less stable product is not always the faster product

## 20.3 UV-visible spectroscopy
![Pasted image 20260601225451](../pasted_images/Pasted%20image%2020260601225451.png)
**Beer-Lambert Law:**
$$A=\varepsilon c\mathscr l$$
- $A$ = absorbance (no units)
- $c$ = concentration in molarity (M = mol/L)
- $\mathscr l$ = sample cell length in cm
- $\varepsilon$ = extinction coefficient, unique to each compound
	- Units: $M^{-1}cm^{-1}$
	- Ranges from $0$ to $10^{16}$ $M^{-1}cm^{-1}$
		- $\varepsilon>10^{14}$ = high intensity absorption
		- $\varepsilon<10^{14}$ = low intensity absorption
	- Double-stranded DNA at 260 nm = 6670 $M^{-1}cm^{-1}$
	- ATP at 257 nm = 14700 $M^{-1}cm^{-1}$
****
UV-vis causes electronic transition, typically from the *highest occupied molecular orbital* (**HOMO**) to the *lowest unoccupied molecular orbital* (**LUMO**)

## 20.4 Pericyclic reaction theory
- Involves a cyclic redistribution of bonding electrons
- Electrons move around in a cirlce
- "concerted" = reaction occurs in a single step (no intermediates, one transition state), bond breaks and forms at the same time
### Cycloaddition
![300](../pasted_images/Pasted%20image%2020260601230442.png)
- Lose 2 $\pi$ bonds and gain 2 $\sigma$ bonds
- Sigma are lower lying MO orbitals so rxn is energetically favourable (thermodynamically downhill)
### Electrocyclic reaction
![300](../pasted_images/Pasted%20image%2020260601230433.png)
- Lose 1 $\pi$ bond and gain 1 $\sigma$ bond
- Also energetically favourable
### Sigmatropic rearrangement
![400](../pasted_images/Pasted%20image%2020260601230504.png)
- Lost 1 $\sigma$ bond and gained 1 $\sigma$ bond
- Cope: [3,3]-sigmatropic rearrangement
- Claisen: Generating C=O double bond is energetically favourable

## 20.5 Diels-Alder reaction
[4+2]cycloaddition
Conjugated dienes react with some molecules with double/triple bonds to form two new $\sigma$ bonds and a ring structure
- diene + **dienophile** $\to$ **Dies-Alder adduct**
- $\pi$ bonds are broken and two new stronger $\sigma$ bonds and a $\pi$ bond are formed
![400](../pasted_images/Pasted%20image%2020260601230605.png)

### A. Diene must be able to assume *s-cis* conformation
Diene must be in *s-cis conformation*
- Cyclopentadiene are constrained to be in this formation
![300](../pasted_images/Pasted%20image%2020260601231517.png)
![300](../pasted_images/Pasted%20image%2020260601231525.png)
Note on constitution vs. configuration vs. conformation:
- Constitution: Describes identity of atoms and their connectivity (bond and bond type)
- Configuration: Permanent geometry arising from the spatial arrangement of bonds
- Conformation: Shape obtainable by rotation across a single bond
![300](../pasted_images/Pasted%20image%2020260601231649.png)

### B. Substituent effect
![400](../pasted_images/Pasted%20image%2020260602002325.png)
![Pasted image 20260602002455](../pasted_images/Pasted%20image%2020260602002455.png)
- Dienophile usually has strong EWG (like carbonyls) and the diene has strong ERG.
Kinetic reactions prefer endo orientation
### C. Bicyclic products
![Pasted image 20260602005116](../pasted_images/Pasted%20image%2020260602005116.png)
Endo product is favoured when there is an EWG due to the secondary orbital interaction
- Otherwise, Exo product is sterically favoured
![Pasted image 20260602141709](../pasted_images/Pasted%20image%2020260602141709.png)
![Pasted image 20260602004938](../pasted_images/Pasted%20image%2020260602004938.png)
For bicyclic Diels-Alder adducts,
- **exo** substituents are on opposite side of newly formed ring
- **endo** substituents are on the same side

### D. Stereospecificity at both diene and dienophile
- Configuration (E/Z) of dienophile is retained
- Stereospecificity: Starting with stereo-distinct material => end with stereo-distinct products, e.g. geometry of reactant is preserved
	- Stereoselectivity: We get one product even if different stereoisomers in reactant
- Reaction is concerted: Only one transition state => no intermediate, no rotation around single bonds can occur
![Pasted image 20260602003351](../pasted_images/Pasted%20image%2020260602003351.png)
- For dienes:
	- **Outside groups** end up **cis** to the activating group (pointing **down** if the activating group is down).
	- **Inside groups** end up **trans** to the activating group (pointing **up** if the activating group is down).
![Pasted image 20260602004812](../pasted_images/Pasted%20image%2020260602004812.png)
- ortho/para rules are always dictated by the strongest EDG on the diene and the strongest EWG on the dienophile
