# 21.1 Structure of benzene
- Benzene and derivatives are classified as **aromatic**. They are stable and relatively unreactive. The term **arene** is used to describe aromatic hydrocarbons.
## A. Kekule's model of benzene
- Each ring contains three double bonds that shift back and forth so rapidly that each form cannot be separated
- This interconversion is an equilibrium
![200](../pasted_images/Pasted%20image%2020260420094853.png)
## B. Molecular orbital model of benzene
- Each C is sp2 hybridized
- Each C makes $\pi$ bonds by sp2-sp2 overlap with adjacent C, and sp2-1s overlap with a hydrogen atom
- Each C has a single unhybridized 2p orbital containing 1 electron
- The C-C bond is an intermediate between a double and single bond
![200](../pasted_images/Pasted%20image%2020260420095110.png)
- Lowest energy configuration of orbitals gives a torus-shape above and below the ring
![400](../pasted_images/Pasted%20image%2020260420095232.png)
## C. Resonance model of benzene
- Benzene's actual structure is a hybrid of two equivalent contributing structures
![200](../pasted_images/Pasted%20image%2020260420095443.png)
- Benzene has a large **resonance energy** (difference in energy between a resonance hybrid and the most stable contributing structure)

![300](../pasted_images/Pasted%20image%2020260427103639.png)
- We can estimate the resonance energy by comparing the heats of hydrogenation of cyclohexene and benzene:
	- Cyclohexene + H2/Ni $\to$ Cyclohexane, $\Delta H^\circ=-29.6$
	- "Cyclohexatriene" + H2/Ni $\to$ Cyclohexane, $\Delta H^\circ=-95.9$
	- Benzene + H2/Ni $\to$ Cyclohexane, $\Delta H^\circ=-49.8$
		- 36 kcal/mol more stable
- Reducing the benzene to cyclohexane took much more energy than just reducing 3 alkenes ("cyclohexatriene")
# 21.2 Aromaticity
- Unusually stable
- High saturation index
## A. Huckel criteria for aromaticity
1) Be **cyclic**
2) Have one p orbital per ring atom
3) The molecule is **planar** to allow overlap of 2p orbitals
4) There must be a closed loop of $(4n+2)$ $\pi$ electrons in the aromatic $\pi$ system
### Frost circles
Inscribed polygon method
- Inscribe the ring polygon in a circle such that one of its vertices is at the bottom. Then the relative energy of the $\pi$ orbitals are given directly by the vertices of the polygon.
![500](../pasted_images/Pasted%20image%2020260420110300.png)
![500](../pasted_images/Pasted%20image%2020260427103724.png)
![500](../pasted_images/Pasted%20image%2020260427103735.png)
![300](../pasted_images/Pasted%20image%2020260427103744.png)
## B. Aromatic hydrocarbons
An **annulene** is a *planar*, *cyclic* hydrocarbon that contains continuously *alternating* double/single bonds
- Cyclobutadiene = [4]annulene
- Benzene = [6]annulene
- Some annulenes need C-C trans bonds to achieve planarity
- [8]annulene: Nonaromatic
- [10]annulene: Puckered shape due to the two inside Hs, and is thus *nonaromatic*
	- Bridging the two carbons instead with a 1-C bridge restores aromaticity
- [12]annulene: Not planar - nonaromatic
- [14]annulene: Aromatic
- [16]annulene: Nonaromatic
- [18]annulene: Aromatic
## C. Antiaromatic hydrocarbons
Some hydrocarbons are *antiaromatic* because they are much less stable than their acyclic analogue with the same number of $\pi$ electrons (unusually unstable)
1) Cyclic
2) One p orbital per ring atom
3) Planar
4) Antiaromatic hydrocarbons have $4n$ instead of $(4n+2)$ $\pi$ electrons

Ex. Cyclobutadiene has four 2p orbitals, giving one filled bonding $\pi$ orbital and two half-filled degenerate nonbonding orbitals
- These two unpaired electrons make it much more reactive
[cyclobutadiene]
![400](../pasted_images/Pasted%20image%2020260420112546.png)

- To avoid antiaromaticity, larger potentially antiaromatic structures adopt a more nonplanar geometry with alternating double/single bonds with different lengths
Ex. Cyclooctatetraene adopts a "tub" conformation, and reacts like an alkene. This way it is just "nonaromatic" instead of "antiaromatic".

## D. Heterocyclic aromatic compounds
- A **heterocyclic compound** contains more than one kind of atom in a ring
- Can be aromatic if the Huckel criteria are met
![400](../pasted_images/Pasted%20image%2020260424001701.png)
![Pasted image 20260427103849](../pasted_images/Pasted%20image%2020260427103849.png)
## E. Aromatic hydrocarbon ions
Any monocyclic unsaturated hydrocarbon with an *odd* number of carbons must have at least one CH2 group and cannot be aromatic: There is no 2p orbital to form a closed loop of 2p orbitals.
![200](../pasted_images/Pasted%20image%2020260428185324.png)
However if the CH2 group is deprotonated to become a CH+ group, the C is now sp2 hybridized with a vacant 2p orbital (still containing only 2 e-), it forms an aromatic cation.
![500](../pasted_images/Pasted%20image%2020260428185338.png)

However, the following reaction does not occur because the cation would be antiaromatic (only 4 pi electrons).
![Pasted image 20260428185543](../pasted_images/Pasted%20image%2020260428185543.png)
Instead we can convert the CH2 group into a CH- group, in which the carbon becomes sp2 hybridized and has two electrons.
![Pasted image 20260428185833](../pasted_images/Pasted%20image%2020260428185833.png)
Cyclopentadiene is the most acidic hydrocarbon because of the extreme stability of the aromatic conjugate base.
![400](../pasted_images/Pasted%20image%2020260428190430.png)

Named as derivatives of benzene, but often have IUPAC common names.
![Pasted image 20260428221247](../pasted_images/Pasted%20image%2020260428221247.png)

In molecules with other functional groups, it is named as a phenyl substituent.
![300](../pasted_images/Pasted%20image%2020260428221336.png)
Substituent-only groups:
F- (fluoro-), Cl- (chloro-), I- (iodo-), -NO2 (nitro-), -N=O (nitroso-)
## B. Disubstituted benzenes
When one substituent imparts a special name to the compound, it is named as a derivative of that compound with that cxarbon treated as #1.
![300](../pasted_images/Pasted%20image%2020260428221515.png)
*Cresol*
Otherwise, if neither inparts a special name, we list the substituents in alphabetical order, with the lowest alphabetically ranking substituent at carbon #1.
![300](../pasted_images/Pasted%20image%2020260428221558.png)
## C. Polysubstituted benzenes
If any substituent imparts a special name, name it as a derivative of that compound with that carbon as #1. Then list all the others in alphabetical order, numbered so that the *total sum* is minimized.
![300](../pasted_images/Pasted%20image%2020260428221802.png)
If none of the substituents impart a special name, number to minimize the sum and then list them alphabetically, followed by benzene.
![350](../pasted_images/Pasted%20image%2020260428221813.png)

# 21.4 Phenols
## A. Structure and nomenclature
Named as derivatives of phenols, or by common names.
![500](../pasted_images/Pasted%20image%2020260502141824.png)
![500](../pasted_images/Pasted%20image%2020260502141834.png)
## B. Acidity
Phenols are significantly more acidic than alcohols
The phenoxide ion is far more stable than the alkoxide ion because the negative charge can be delocalized by resonance
The negative charge can be placed on the oxygen, and then the ortho and para positions on the ring
![500](../pasted_images/Pasted%20image%2020260502140337.png)
![500](../pasted_images/Pasted%20image%2020260502141851.png)
### Substituents
Alkyl substituted phenols are weaker acids
- The aromatic ring is more electronegative than the alkyl substituent, so they have an *electron-releasing* effect, destabilizing the phenoxide ion resonance
![500](../pasted_images/Pasted%20image%2020260502141952.png)
![400](../pasted_images/Pasted%20image%2020260502140758.png)
Halogen substituents are *electron-withdrawing* and stabilize the ion, making it more acidic.
![500](../pasted_images/Pasted%20image%2020260502142009.png)
Nitrophenols exhibit both an inductive and resonance effect, further strengthening the acid.
- The effect is greatest in the *para* position because the negative charge can be delocalized onto the oxygen of the nitro group
![400](../pasted_images/Pasted%20image%2020260502140737.png)
![500](../pasted_images/Pasted%20image%2020260502142022.png)

## C. Acid-base reactions of phenols
Phenols are weak acids and react with strong bases like NaOH to form water-soluble salts.
![400](../pasted_images/Pasted%20image%2020260502141000.png)
Most phenols do not react with weaker bases.
![400](../pasted_images/Pasted%20image%2020260502141027.png)
Phenols (weakly acidic) can be separated from alcohols (neutral) in this way.

# 21.5 Reactions at benzylic position
The benzylic position is an sp3-hybridized carbon bonded to a benzene ring
![200](../pasted_images/Pasted%20image%2020260502143504.png)
The benzylic position is *highly reactive* because the benzylic cation and radical intermediates are resonance stabilized by delocalization into the ring
![Pasted image 20260502144230](../pasted_images/Pasted%20image%2020260502144230.png)
![Pasted image 20260504101746](../pasted_images/Pasted%20image%2020260504101746.png)
## A. Oxidation
When toulene is treated with strong oxidization agents like **H2CrO4** and **KMnO4**, the methyl group is oxidized to a carboxyl group, giving benzoic acid.
Ethyl and isopropyl side chains are also oxidized to carbonyl groups (since they have one benzylic hydrogen). All other groups are removed as CO2. 
![500](../pasted_images/Pasted%20image%2020260504101759.png)
Halogen and nitro substituents on the ring are unaffected.
![300](../pasted_images/Pasted%20image%2020260504101912.png)
Note that *tert*-butylbenzene is not oxidized as there is no benzylic hydrogen.
![300](../pasted_images/Pasted%20image%2020260504101824.png)
If more than one alkyl side chain e xists, each is oxidized to -COOH.
![600](../pasted_images/Pasted%20image%2020260504101840.png)
![Pasted image 20260504101856](../pasted_images/Pasted%20image%2020260504101856.png)

## B. Halogenation
Benzylic hydrogens can be replaced by bromine or chlorine 
![Pasted image 20260504102114](../pasted_images/Pasted%20image%2020260504102114.png)

### Mechanism
Halogenation of benzylic position via radical chain mecanism.
- X2 converted to two radicals
- X* abstracts the benzylic hydrogen to form resonance-stabilized benzylic radical
- Benzylic radical reacts with another molecule of X2 to give product and X* to continue the chain reaction
- NBS used as source of Br2
 ![300](../pasted_images/Pasted%20image%2020260504102135.png)
![Pasted image 20260504102157](../pasted_images/Pasted%20image%2020260504102157.png)
### Regioselectivity
Halogenation with larger alkyl side chain is highly regioselective, due to the resonance stabilization of the benzylic radical intermediate
Bromination is more regioselective for benzylic position than chlorination
![400](../pasted_images/Pasted%20image%2020260504102219.png)
Order of stability of radicals reflects the C-H bond dissociation enthalpies for formation of these radicals
![300](../pasted_images/Pasted%20image%2020260502150545.png)

**Hammond postulate**
The structure of the transition state is more similar to the starting material (reactants) if the reaction is *exothermic*
The structure of the transition state is more similar to the products if the reaction is *endothermic*
![400](../pasted_images/Pasted%20image%2020260502164128.png)
Chlorine radical is a more reactive species (exothermic reaction)
- Energy difference in transition state is smaller
HBr bond is weaker, so the reaction requires more energy since Br is less reactive (endothermic reaction)
![Pasted image 20260504102233](../pasted_images/Pasted%20image%2020260504102233.png)
## C. Hydrogenolysis of benzyl ethers
Benzylic ethers are cleaved with catalytic hydrogenation
Gives toulene + alcohol
![400](../pasted_images/Pasted%20image%2020260502150706.png)

The C-O bond between the benzylic carbon and the oxygen is cleaved and replaced with a C-H bond
![Pasted image 20260504101402](../pasted_images/Pasted%20image%2020260504101402.png)
Benzyl ethers can serve as protecting groups for -OH in alcohols/phenols
