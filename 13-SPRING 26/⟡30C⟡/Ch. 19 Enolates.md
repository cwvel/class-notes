# 19.2 Aldol reaction
An enolate anion adds to the carbonyl of another aldehyde or ketone, forming a new C-C bond.
It produces an **aldol:** a compound that is both an *aldehyde* and an *alcohol*, either a $\beta$-hydroxy-aldehyde or $\beta$-hydroxy-ketone.
- A carbonyl with a hydroxyl on the $\beta$-carbon
![Pasted image 20260401143329](Pasted%20image%2020260401143329.png)
## Base-catalysed aldol reaction
![Pasted image 20260401143439](Pasted%20image%2020260401143439.png)
1. **Removal of $\alpha$-hydrogen** gives resonance-stabilised *enolate anion* and forms H2O
2. **Addition to C=O** of another aldehyde/ketone forms C-C bond
3. **Add a proton** from H2O, regenerating the base
## Acid-catalysed aldol reaction
![Pasted image 20260401143833](Pasted%20image%2020260401143833.png)
1. **Keto-enol taut.** of aldehyde/ketone occurs in acidic conditions
2. **Add a proton** from the acid to the oxygen of another molecule of the aldehyde/ketone gives the cation nucleophile (oxonium ion)
3. **Addition to C=O.** Enol attacks the protonated C=O+ forms C-C bond
4. **Deprotonation** by A- regenerates the acid catalyst and gives product
***
In the base-catalysed reaction, the enolate *anion* (nucleophile) attacks the *neutral* carbonyl (electrophile).
In the acid-catalysed reaction, the *neutral* enol (nucleophile) attacks the protonated C=O+ *cation* (electrophile).
Note that the products are formed as racemic ($\pm$) mixtures.
## Crossed aldol reaction
Each possible enolate ion can add to each unique carbonyl, forming a complex mixture of products.
![](Pasted%20image%2020260401144502.png#center|600)
The reaction is only useful (produces one main product) if one of the carbonyls **does not bear an $\alpha$-hydrogen**
- e.g. formaldehyde. Then the enolate adds to formaldehyde much more readily than to itself (less hindered)
![](Pasted%20image%2020260401172428.png#center|600)
Additionally, if one side is more sterically hindered than the other, (since LDA is very bulky) there will only be one enolate formed.
## Intramolecular aldol reaction
5- and 6-membered rings form more readily because they are the most stable.
$$6>4,\quad 5>7$$
- Smaller rings form faster than larger rings because reacting groups are closer, but 3- and 4-membered rings are disfavored because of ring strain.
![](Pasted%20image%2020260401172600.png#center|500)
- Can also lead to dehydration to form $\alpha,\beta$-unsaturated ketone
	- In acidic conditions, first **tautomerize** keto->enol, **protonate** leaving group, then **enol lone pair** kicks down followed by H2O leaving
![Pasted image 20260401172747](Pasted%20image%2020260401172747.png)
Additionally, fused rings are favored over bridged rings.
![](Pasted%20image%2020260401172608.png#center|500)
## Nitro-aldol (Henry) reaction
Acts as an enolate, but with a (+) nitrogen instead of a carbon, and a (-) oxygen associated with it
![](IMG_2964.jpeg#center|500)
1. -OH deprotonates, forming a double bond: pKa of the nitro enolate < pKa of H2O, equilibrium lies to the right
2. Same alkyl attack on C=O of another ketone/aldehyde, followed by protonation

## Aldol-dehydration
Always acid catalysed?
1. **Tautomerization** of keto to enol
2. **Protonation** of the leaving group (-OH alcohol group)
3. **Deprotonation** of enol, -OH group leaves and $\alpha$-$\beta$ C=C bond forms
![Pasted image 20260405233106](Pasted%20image%2020260405233106.png)
Forms $\alpha$,$\beta$-unsaturated ketone/aldehyde.

# 19.9 Directed aldol reaction
A **directed aldol reaction** is a controlled version of the aldol reaction used to selectively form a specific $\beta$-hydroxy-carbonyl (aldol product) by pre-forming a particular enolate.
- In a standard aldol reaction, multiple products can form
![Pasted image 20260401174007](Pasted%20image%2020260401174007.png)
LDA is a strong base that reacts with acetone (both $\alpha$-hydrogens identical) to give a single enolate species: lithium enolate
- Typically in a polar, aprotic solvent at low temperatures ($-85^\circ$ C)
- Nearly all the acetone is converted to enolate, the self-adol reaction (acetone + enolate) is suppressed
![Pasted image 20260401174341](Pasted%20image%2020260401174341.png)
The enolate anion can then selectively react with the specific electrophile added next. 
## Formation of LDA
![](Pasted%20image%2020260401174741.png#center|500)
Deprotonation of **diisopropylamine** with an organolithium, **n-butyllithium**
- Note that we can't directly use n-BuLi (strong base) to deprotonate the acetone because it can also add to carbonyls, forming a mixture of products. LDA cannot due to steric hinderance from the isopropyl groups.
**LDA is very sensitive to sterics:**
![](Pasted%20image%2020260401175018.png#center|400)
Note that if one less than 1 equivalent of LDA is added, only a fraction of the carbonyl compound is deprotonated and a significant amount of neutral carbonyl remains to act as an acid, leading to thermodynamic equilibrium.

# 19.3 Claisen and Dieckmann condensations
**Claisen condensation:** Two ester molecules react in base to give a *$\beta$-ketoester product*.
**Dieckmann condensation:** An intramolecular Claisen condensation of a *diester*.
![](Pasted%20image%2020260402123617.png#center|220)
## Claisen condensation mechanism
Requires an ester with two $\alpha$-hydrogens (one to form initial enolate anion, and one to form the enolate ion of the resulting $\beta$-ketoester)
The base used must be nonaqueous, otherwise it would result in hydrolysis of the ester.
![Pasted image 20260405233123](Pasted%20image%2020260405233123.png)
1. **Deprotonatation** of $\alpha$-hydrogen on ester by base gives enolate anion. Equilibrium lies *to the left* so amount of enolate formed is very low (excess ester)
2. **Enolate anion attacks** the C=O of another ester, forming tetrahedral intermediate
3. **RO- group leaves** as electron pair on O collapses down
4. **Deprotonation** of $\beta$-ketoester by RO- group to give the anion
5. **Reprotonate** by adding water, giving the $\beta$-ketoester

## Dieckmann condensation
Intramolecular Claisen condensation to give 5- or 6-membered ring.
![Pasted image 20260405233134](Pasted%20image%2020260405233134.png)

## Crossed Claisen condensations
With two different esters, each with two different $\alpha$-hydrogens, a mixture of four $\beta$-ketoesters is possible. 
The reaction is only useful if one of the esters has no $\alpha$-hydrogens (can only function as the enolate ion acceptor).

## Hydrolysis and decarboxylation
Transforms $\beta$-ketoesters into a ketone through removal of the ester group. A β-ketoester has a ketone and an ester separated by one carbon.
1. **Saponification.** Under acidic or basic conditions, the ester is hydrolyzed, forming a $\beta$-keto acid.
2. **Decarboxylation** under heating causes loss of CO2, resulting in a ketone.
![Pasted image 20260405233146](Pasted%20image%2020260405233146.png)

# 19.6 Acetoacetic ester synthesis
$\beta$-ketoesters are versatile starting materials for forming new C-C bonds.
- $\alpha$-hydrogens between two C=O groups are very acidic
![](Pasted%20image%2020260405233212.png#center|300)
- The enolate anion following the loss of the $\alpha$-hydrogen is very nucleophilic
- The product can undergo decarboxylation after hydrolysis of the ester
![Pasted image 20260405233229](Pasted%20image%2020260405233229.png)

1. **Deprotonation.** Very acidic $\alpha$-hydrogens means ethyl acetoacetate is converted completely to its anion.
2. **SN2 with halo-alkanes.** The enolate anion is a nucleophile and attacks methyl and primary haloalklanes, $\alpha$-haloketones, $\alpha$-haloesters. *Secondary gives lower yields and tertiary undergoes E2 instead.*
3. **Hydrolysis of ester.** NaOH with acid workup gives $\beta$-ketoacid.
4. **Decarboxylation.** Add heat to remove CO2, giving the alkylated product.
Product pattern is $\alpha$-alkylated ketone: carbonyl compound with $\alpha$-carbon with 1-2 groups
# 19.7 Malonic ester synthesis
Analogous to acetoacetic aester synthesis:
1. Malonic ester completely converted to enolate anion with base
2. Enolate anion acts as nucleophile in SN2 reaction
3. Alkylated acetoacetic ester hydrolyzed and acidified to give an alkylated acetoacetic acid (COOH group)
4. Heating causes decarboxylation to give product

# 19.5 Enamines
Enamines have a C=C bond adjacent to a C-N bond, e.g.
$$R_2C=C-NR_2$$
The $\beta$-carbon is a nucleophile due to the C=C bond and electron pair on N.

## Formation
Primary amine with carbonyl forms imines or Schiff base:
![Pasted image 20260406142751](Pasted%20image%2020260406142751.png)
Enamines are formed by reacting a *secondary* amine with an aldehyde or ketone.
![Pasted image 20260406142804](Pasted%20image%2020260406142804.png)
## Enamine selectivity
Stability preferences:
![Pasted image 20260406142817](Pasted%20image%2020260406142817.png)
- More substituted, conjugated system
- Outcome is controlled less by alkene substitution stability and more by *steric constraints*:
- Allylic 1-3 strain: Steric clash between substituents on the $\alpha$-carbon and substituents on nitrogen, held in planar by alkene

## Alkylation
Enamines can be **alkylated** on $\beta$-carbon in SN2 with haloalkanes.
- Compared to enolate anions, enamines are less basic and favor the substitution reaction over elimination (cleaner, more product).
![Pasted image 20260406143301](Pasted%20image%2020260406143301.png)
1. **Nucleophilic attack.** Electron pair on N kicks down and C=C bond attacks the electrophile.
2. **Hydrolysis.** Addition of aqueous acid gives ketone (C=O group).
## Acylation
Enamines can be **acylated** on $\beta$-carbon with acid chlorides/anhydrides (nucleophilic acyl substitution).

# 19.8 Conjugate addition to $\alpha,\beta$-unsaturated carbonyl compounds
## Michael reaction
**Michael reaction.** The $\beta$-carbon of an $\alpha,\beta$-unsaturated carbonyl is electrophilic and can react with enolate ions (called a *Michael acceptor*)
- This is because an important resonance-contributing structure has a (+) at the $\beta$-carbon
- The product of this reaction has oxygen in a **1,5 arrangement** (aldol and claisen give 1,3)
![](Pasted%20image%2020260406103212.png#center|400)
![](Pasted%20image%2020260406103219.png#center|400)
![Pasted image 20260406143619](Pasted%20image%2020260406143619.png)
### Mechanism:
1. **Enolate anion** formed in base
2. **Nu. attack.** Enolate attacks $\beta$-carbon and electrons shift onto C=O, generating enolate intermediate
3. **Protonation** gives enol and regenerates base, then **tautomerize**s to keto form (shown in concerted step)
![Pasted image 20260406143638](Pasted%20image%2020260406143638.png)
![Pasted image 20260406143649](Pasted%20image%2020260406143649.png)
- Michael donors are typically weak bases
![Pasted image 20260406143707](Pasted%20image%2020260406143707.png)
## Robinson annulation
**Robinson annulation.** *Michael reaction* with $\alpha,\beta$-unsaturated ketone followed by *intramolecular aldol reaction*, creating a *cyclic product* (6-membered rings).
Gilman reagents undergo conjugate addition similarly to Michael reaction.
![](Pasted%20image%2020260408130026.png#center|400)
(Cyclic) ketones, $\beta$-ketoesters, $\beta$-diketones + $\alpha,\beta$-enones $\longrightarrow$ **cyclohexenone**

### Mechanism
1. **Michael reaction**
	1. Form enolate anion in base
	2. Attack on $\beta$-carbon, forming enolate
2. **Intramolecular aldol**
	1. Enolate attacks its own C=O group, closing 6-membered ring
3. **Dehydration**
	1. Base deprotonates $\alpha$-carbon (between hydroxyl and carbonyl groups)
	2. $\alpha,\beta$ C=C bond forms, and -OH leaves, regenerating the base

### 1,2- vs. 1,4-Addition
Why is the less stable product favored?
**Strong base (alkyl lithium, Grignard) preferably provides 1,2-addition poroduct**
	1. 1,2-addition is faster (transitional state lower energy). Why? Lewis acidic Li+ becomes associated with electronegative lone pair on oxygen O:, so Me- ends up closer to C=O
	2. Alkyl lithium are very strong bases, high energy species - much higher compared to the addition product. There is not enough energy to make it back to the starting reactants (1,2-addition is irreversible due to high energy MeLi) so directly depends mostly on speed of reaction rather than equilibrium *(kinetically controlled reaction)*
![Pasted image 20260408213406](Pasted%20image%2020260408213406.png)
**Weak base (Michael donors) favors 1,4-addition product**
1. 1,2 product has higher energy, so 1,2=addition is more reversible. This is because now the base is more stable (lower energy). *(thermodynamically controlled reaction)*
